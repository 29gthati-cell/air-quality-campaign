Clear the Air: The Invisible Threat

Air pollution is not just the smoke we see—it is an urgent public health and environmental crisis happening every single day.

Statement of the Problem

Air pollution is not just the smoke we see in the air—it also includes tiny, invisible particles called PM2.5 that can enter our lungs and even our bloodstream every single day (WHO, 2024).

Global & Local Challenges

Air pollution can cause both sudden asthma attacks and serious long-term health problems like heart disease. Children are especially vulnerable because their lungs are still developing, and they inhale more air per unit body weight than adults (Lung Association, 2026).

Why This Matters Now

According to the WHO (2024), air pollution causes about 4.2 million premature deaths each year. It is also an issue of environmental justice, because communities of color are 2.4 times more likely to live in areas with high levels of pollution.

The Hidden Price Tag: Air pollution affects more than just our health—it also places a huge burden on the global economy, costing about $8.1 trillion each year in lost productivity and healthcare expenses.

Proposed Solutions

To address this critical global issue, we propose a dual-action framework designed to clean our air through structural change and natural defense systems:

  1. Transitioning to Electric Public Transit: This structural shift significantly reduces localized vehicle emissions, drastically improving the immediate air quality that urban populations breathe daily.
  2. Creating "Green Zones": Establishing urban forests and tree-filled community spaces allows nature to act as a physical filter, lowering pollution levels across neighborhoods.
Proven Results: These solutions have already shown real results. Cities like Bogotá reduced pollutants by 20% through electrification (Clean Air Fund, 2026). Furthermore, urban trees can remove up to 24% of particulate matter in the immediate areas surrounding them.

Take Action Today

We are executing a three-part campaign plan to mobilize change:

• Educate: Teach communities how to read and respond to the Air Quality Index (AQI).
• Reduce: Encourage individuals to swap just one weekly car trip for walking, biking, or public transit.
• Grow: Partner with local non-profits to plant native trees in highly impacted neighborhoods.
